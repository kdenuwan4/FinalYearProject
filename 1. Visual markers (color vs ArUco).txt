1. Visual markers (color vs ArUco)

Your reasoning here is solid, and more importantly, it shows real engineering awareness.

Color tracking looks simple at first, but in practice it fails in exactly the ways you predicted: lighting changes shift color values, shadows distort perception, and reflections introduce noise. In a controlled lab demo you might get it working, but in a real evaluation setting it becomes unreliable.

ArUco markers solve all of that in a fundamentally different way. They are not just “patterns” — they are mathematically encoded squares that OpenCV can robustly detect regardless of moderate lighting variation. More importantly, they give you three critical things at once:

Position (x, y)
Orientation (rotation of the marker)
Unique identity (ID)

That combination is exactly what your system needs. Without orientation, your robot can’t decide how to turn properly. Without ID, you can’t scale to multiple robots cleanly.

So this part of your assessment is not just correct — it’s a design decision you should lock permanently.

2. A* vs dynamic multi-robot reality

You correctly identified a deeper issue here: A* assumes a static world, but your robots are moving obstacles.

Your proposed solution (Cooperative A*) is academically correct — but here’s the important distinction:

Something being correct does not mean it is the right engineering choice.

Cooperative A* introduces a time dimension and turns your grid into a space-time problem. That significantly increases complexity, both in implementation and debugging. For a final-year project, this can easily consume all your time without giving proportional benefit.

A better approach — and this is what experienced engineers would do — is to separate global planning from local coordination.

Think of it like this:

A* gives each robot a desired path in an ideal world
A coordination layer enforces real-world safety

So instead of making A* smarter, you keep it simple and add intelligence around it.

For example, if two robots are predicted to come too close, you don’t recompute everything in space-time. You simply pause one robot based on a priority rule. That gives you most of the benefit with a fraction of the complexity.

So your insight was correct — but the better engineering decision is to not over-solve the problem.

3. Vision latency and overshoot

This is actually one of the strongest points you raised. Most student projects completely ignore this.

You correctly noticed that there is a delay chain:
camera → processing → decision → transmission → robot action

That delay means your system is always reacting to the past, not the present.

Your proposed solution (Kalman filter) is again theoretically strong, but here’s the reality:

A Kalman filter is powerful, but it requires:

a motion model
noise modeling
tuning

If any of those are off, it can perform worse than a simple method.

A more practical starting point is to think in terms of control strategy instead of prediction complexity.

Instead of commanding “go to this exact cell,” you command small incremental actions and constantly correct. This reduces overshoot naturally because the robot never builds large momentum toward a distant goal.

If needed, you can then add a simple velocity-based prediction (using previous position changes). This gives you most of the benefit of prediction without the overhead of a full Kalman filter.

So your intuition is advanced — the key is to match solution complexity to project scope.

4. Missing fallback logic

This is not just correct — it’s essential for a system like yours.

Right now your design includes intelligent decision-making, but intelligence without safety is dangerous in engineering systems.

You correctly identified situations where things can go wrong:

tracking fails (camera loses robot)
conflicting decisions
uncertain outputs

In all these cases, the system must not “guess.” It must fall back to a safe state.

The correct mindset here is:

If the system is unsure, it must stop.

This is exactly how real autonomous systems are designed. You don’t try to be clever when confidence drops — you switch to a safe mode.

Also, this fallback should exist at two levels:

Central controller (what you’re building)
Robot-level safety (handled by Member 2)

That layered safety is what makes your system robust.
